§01 — Selected studies

Six studies, all of them shipped findings.

Each study is summarised as a one-line finding, the question it answered, and the decision it informed. Full reports are available under NDA.

Diary study3 weeksn=24

Why finance teams read the ledger out loud.

Northbeam's bookkeepers said the close week was painful; we recorded twenty-four of them doing it. The thing nobody had documented: they read journals out loud, one column at a time, to a partner.

The ledger needed to be readable like a sentence, not scannable like a sheet. Three quarters later, support tickets in close week dropped 38%.

Field interviews2 weeksn=18

What an onboarding flow is competing with.

Field & Format wanted to know why activation was flat. Eighteen first-run sessions, all in the user's own context, showed activation wasn't the problem — it was the seventy-two hours afterwards.

Activation looked fine in metrics; second-session intent had collapsed. We redesigned day-three, not day-zero.
Field & Format2025Solo
Usability + ledger5 weeksn=12

Twelve sessions on a data table.

Atlas Group's admin table had three rounds of redesign and no data on which one worked. We ran twelve moderated sessions, recorded keystrokes, and mapped the rotation of attention.

The new column was being looked at twice as often and acted on half as much. The problem was trust, not visibility.

Surveys + interviews4 weeksn=212 / 22

Mapping the actual jobs to be done for a writing app.

Bureau Verso's writers self-described very differently from how they wrote. We ran a 212-person survey and a 22-person follow-up, then plotted the surveyed roles against the actual time-on-task.

Three jobs accounted for 84% of paid usage. The product had spent two years building for a fourth.

§02 — Methods

How a study is built.

I don't pick a method until the question is sharp. These are the three I run most often, and the conditions I run them under.

M-01

Generative interviews

When the team doesn't yet know what to build, or has built it and isn't sure why.

  • n = 12 — 24 participants
  • Mostly in the user's context
  • Recorded, transcribed, coded
  • Findings as one-pagers, not decks
M-02

Diary studies

When you need to see something across days — close week, weekend usage, three-week rituals.

  • 14 — 28 day duration
  • Daily prompts, light burden
  • Mid-study debrief at day 7
  • Mixed quant + qual end-state survey
M-03

Evaluative sessions

When a feature is built or near-built and the team wants to know what it gets right and where it strains.

  • n = 8 — 14 participants
  • Moderated, task-based
  • Live notes with the build team
  • Severity-scored findings

§05 — Field notes

Notes from the field.

Short pieces, written while studies are running. Subscribers receive one a fortnight.

Field 2026 · 05 · 12 — Kyoto

What people do before the screen.

Three of yesterday's interviews started with the participant clearing physical paper off the desk. I never coded for that before. I might start.

Field 2026 · 04 · 03 — Berlin

On not running a survey first.

Two clients in a row asked for a survey to "calibrate" the qualitative work. Both surveys would have asked the wrong questions. Sequence matters more than mix.

Note 2026 · 02 · 22 — Kyoto

A short defence of fewer participants.

If your study makes you change your mind by participant six, run six more, not sixty. The next finding is usually a refinement, not a new direction.

Note 2025 · 11 · 30 — Berlin

The deliverable nobody reads.

The 80-slide report is for the researcher, not the team. Replace it with three one-pagers and a 20-minute briefing — the findings will travel further by Friday.
