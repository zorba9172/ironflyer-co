Assume breach. Ship anyway.

Vaultline assumes the laptop is compromised, the CI runner is compromised, and the intern's account is compromised. Every secret has a blast radius. Every retrieval is signed. Every leak is contained.

01 · scope

Per-secret blast radius

Every secret declares what it can touch. A leaked staging key cannot reach prod. A leaked prod read cannot become a write.

02 · sign

Signed retrievals

Every fetch is signed with a hardware-bound key. Replays are rejected. The audit log shows which workload, which commit, which user.

03 · ttl

Short lifetimes by default

One-hour TTLs are the default, not a feature. Long-lived credentials require a written justification — and they decay anyway.

04 · break

Break-glass with a ledger

Emergency access is one click. It also pages your security team, posts to the audit channel, and rolls the credential at the end of the window.

05 · scan

Leak scanner that closes the loop

Vaultline watches GitHub, your CI logs, and your SIEM. A leaked secret is auto-rotated within 60 seconds and replaced everywhere it lives.

06 · siem

Audit log your auditor reads

Every event ships to your SIEM as structured JSON. Human-readable. Court-readable. Sealed with a tamper-evident checksum chain.

// blast radius

Every secret knows how far it can burn.

A Vaultline secret carries its scope inside it. The blast radius is checked at retrieval time, at use time, and at rotation time. If a credential leaks, the damage stops at the radius — not at the perimeter.

No more "this read-only key happens to also have admin." Scopes are signed. Scopes are visible. Scopes are auditable.
