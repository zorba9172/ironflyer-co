The cover story

A small roaster in Trondheim, and the case for staying small.

Tora Lillehammer roasts five kilograms at a time, four mornings a week, and turns away wholesale at the door. The numbers don't quite work. The coffee is the best in the country. A long conversation about scale, attention, and what gets lost when a roaster grows up.

By Idun Sjølie · Issue 31 · 14 minute read

Tasting notes

What we drank this month

Eight coffees, cupped blind, talked about for too long, and written up with as little flourish as we could manage.

Tim Wendelboe · Oslo

Finca Tamana, washed

Clean, lifted, a long finish that lands on dried apricot. The kind of cup that makes you suspect you have been drinking coffee wrong for years.

ColombiaCaturra1,830 m
Coffee Collective · Copenhagen

Konga Sakaro, natural

Blueberry without theatre. A heavy body, an oily middle, and the slow honeyed close of a roast that knew exactly when to stop.

EthiopiaHeirloom1,950 m
The Barn · Berlin

Kiamabara AA, washed

Blackcurrant on the nose, tomato-leaf in the middle, a bright acidity that holds for a long minute. A coffee for people who like the conversation to keep moving.

KenyaSL281,720 m
Manhattan Coffee · Rotterdam

Sumava, anaerobic honey

Pear cider, a hint of rosewater, a cocoa finish you could chew. Polarising at the cupping table. We came back to it three times.

A letter from the editor

"We will keep the journal small, the writing long, and the coffee honest."

Roastery Notes is a quarterly print magazine and a slow website. We do not run advertising, do not accept review samples, and pay every farm we visit for their time. Readers fund the work directly. That is the whole economy of this place.

If you want to know what specialty coffee actually tastes like in a year, subscribe. We will mail you the print issue, and a small letter every Sunday morning with what we have been drinking.

— Idun Sjølie, editor in chief
