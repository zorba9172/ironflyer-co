The slow study of looking.

Curator Academy is a small online school for art history and curatorial studies. We teach the discipline of looking, writing, and shaping an exhibition — with working curators, not career instructors.

Programs

Three programs, taught slowly.

Each program runs twice a year, capped at thirty students. You write, you read, you visit a museum every week. We meet on Tuesday and Thursday evenings.

i.

The History of Looking

A foundational survey of European and global art from antiquity through the early twentieth century. Designed for students entering the field, with no prerequisites beyond curiosity.

16 weeksFoundation
ii.

Curatorial Practice

How to read a collection, choose a thesis, and shape an exhibition. Students propose, defend, and partially install a virtual show by the end of term.

20 weeksIntermediate
iii.

Writing on Art

A workshop on the catalogue essay, the wall label, the review, and the press release. Weekly drafts, weekly critique, no AI prose.

12 weeksAdvanced

FAQ

Questions before you apply.

Is the academy accredited?

We are not a university and do not grant degrees. Our diploma is a recognized credential in the curatorial community, particularly within European institutions where several of our faculty work.

How much reading is involved?

Roughly 120 pages a week. We assign primary sources and a short selection of secondary literature. Reading is the foundation of the discipline; we do not pretend otherwise.

Are museum visits required?

One museum visit per week is encouraged but not graded. Students unable to visit in person submit a long-form virtual visit using one of our partner institutions' online collections.

Do you accept students without an art history background?

Yes, for the History of Looking program. The other two require either completion of History of Looking or a writing sample for review.

Is financial aid available?

Yes. Up to one third of each cohort receives partial tuition support. Applications are reviewed alongside the academic application.

How are seminars scheduled across time zones?

Live seminars are held Tuesday and Thursday evenings, 6–8 pm Lisbon time. Recordings are posted within twenty-four hours for students who attend asynchronously.

What language are programs taught in?

All programs are taught in English. Primary sources in other languages are provided in translation; advanced students are encouraged to read alongside the original.

Are alumni events offered after a program ends?

Yes. Alumni are invited to four virtual lectures a year and one annual in-person symposium hosted at a partner museum, on a rotating basis between Lisbon, Paris, and New York.

A note

On slowness.

We do not believe one can become a curator in six weeks. We believe one can begin a serious practice — and that the beginning, taken slowly, lasts a long time.
